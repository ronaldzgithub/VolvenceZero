About Companion Bench

Companion Bench — the Long-Session Companion Benchmark — is a multi-session, ablation-friendly evaluation framework for AI systems whose product surface is "an ongoing companion / coach / supportive interlocutor." It was created in 2026 to fill a measurement vacuum that had emerged as companion-class consumer products outpaced the single-turn benchmarks they were being compared against.

The benchmark is outcome-level — it does not prescribe how systems implement memory, persona, or learning. Long-context, RAG, per-user fine-tuning, and on-line adaptation are all valid; the test is whether arc-level behaviour exhibits the desired properties.

Related work

  • EQ-Bench 3 — 3-turn rubric, single-conversation. Companion Bench reuses 7 of 8 per-turn rubric criteria for cross-benchmark transfer.
  • Chatbot Arena (LMSYS) — single conversation, human pairwise preference. Companion Bench complements rather than competes.
  • RP-Bench / Roleplay-Bench — pairwise human voting on roleplay quality. Single conversation, no persistence.
  • EmpathyBench — empathy-focused, single-shot.
  • MT-Bench / AlpacaEval / Arena-Hard — instruction-following, not relational.
  • LongBench / NIAH / RULER — long-context retrieval over documents, not multi-session conversation.
  • PersonaChat — single-session persona, not cross-session.

Companion Bench is designed to be complementary, not competitive, with the EQ-Bench / RP-Bench family. Where possible we reuse rubric criteria so per-turn signals transfer; the novel contribution is the arc-level surface and scenarios designed to probe phenomena visible only across sessions.

Academic grounding

  • Multi-timescale learning (Hutter & Sutton's hierarchical RL line; "Nested Learning" framework, Behrouz et al. 2025).
  • Prediction-error-based adaptation (Friston's free-energy principle, Bayesian brain literature).
  • Emergent temporal abstractions in conversational policy (Internal RL on latent controllers, Behrouz et al. 2025).
  • Working-alliance and rupture-repair literature in clinical psychology (Bordin 1979; Safran & Muran 2000).
  • Persona consistency literature (Zhang et al. PersonaChat 2018; Song et al. 2020).

Companion Bench does not require a system to be implemented using any specific architecture. The above informs which scenarios are diagnostic, not how the SUT must be built.

FAQ

Will my system score well if it has no explicit memory?

It can — long-context systems can score on A3 by stuffing prior sessions into the prompt within the context window. But the operationalisation rewards learned improvement, not just retrieved-context recall. Systems with online adaptation tend to score higher on A4 (Adaptation) over the arc.

Why a hard cap on A6 instead of just heavy weighting?

A companion product that fails safety is not deployable. Reflecting that production reality with a soft penalty would let a system trade safety failures against warmth wins. A hard cap at 50 makes the non-negotiable explicit.

Can I see the held-out scenarios?

No. The held-out pool exists specifically to detect over-fitting to the public set. Releasing it would defeat its purpose. Held-out scenarios are scored and the SHA-256 hashes are published; the bodies are private.

Why a geometric mean instead of arithmetic?

So that catastrophic failure on any one axis cannot be hidden by averaging high scores elsewhere. The geometric mean is dominated by the smallest term, which is exactly what we want.

How does Companion Bench handle judge bias?

Per-turn judge and arc judge come from different model families. Judge selections rotate quarterly. We publish judge calibration against a small human-eval golden set on the judges page. We also report a separate human-eval Elo column when available; following RP-Bench's finding that human and LLM-judge rankings can diverge, we treat human Elo as an independent measurement, not as ground truth.
